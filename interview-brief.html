Interview thesis brief
Make AI dependable enough to operate the business.
Russell Dudek / Director of Product Operations, AI/ML candidate thesis

Actual mandate

Turn ML experimentation into a dependable production capability by building the operating system around deployment automation, observability, performance optimization, risk controls, ownership, and cross-functional decision cadence.

Company-specific hypothesis: make AI dependable enough to support widening digital, commercial, and operational decision surfaces - not a collection of models that work only in notebooks.

Company moment

  • Official role centers on MLOps, model monitoring, optimization, automated tuning, platform operations leadership, cloud, security, risk, and CI/CD.
  • Proof publicly reports 6,000+ daily orders across 32 active markets and 18,000+ products.
  • Southern Glazer's describes advanced automation, connected distribution, tracking, last-mile, and route planning.
  • July 10, 2026 reporting says the company is shifting toward a hybrid field/inside/digital sales model and using AI to adapt.

Operating tensions

  1. Experiment velocity vs. production reliability.
  2. Shared platform standards vs. diverse model/use-case needs.
  3. Automation vs. human intervention authority.
  4. Model metrics vs. business outcomes and adoption.
  5. Platform sophistication vs. operating burden and team adoption.

White-space thesis

AI earns scale when every model carries an operating contract: what it is allowed to influence, how health is measured, who owns intervention, and what evidence justifies the next release.

The contract joins four concerns normally split across teams: intent and ownership; release evidence; live service envelope; intervention rights and learning record.

Proof map

  • Compunetics: high-reliability systems, quality controls, root cause, technical leadership.
  • Amazon: 24/7 visibility, escalation, standard work, operating cadence at scale.
  • Vape-Jet: telemetry-to-action loops across factory, field, customer, support, engineering, and ~1,000 fielded robots.
  • DudeWorth: AI readiness, evaluation, human judgment, escalation, governance rails, adoption and value logic.

Strongest objection

The evidence does not establish seven years of ML platform operations or four years leading that exact function. The degree is also not presented as one of the specified computing/electronics engineering degrees.

Response: do not pretend equivalence. Position the candidacy as reliability and operating-system leadership around a technically deep MLOps organization.

Questions, scorecard, and discovery hypotheses

Decision architecture

Release? Tune? Retrain? Roll back? Fallback? Escalate? Standardize? Stop? The role should make required evidence and intervention authority explicit at each decision.

120-day intent

1-30: read and baseline. 31-60: define Contract v0.1 and rights. 61-90: prove one production path. 91-120: scale reusable controls, operating review, capability plan, and roadmap.

Executive questions

  1. Which model classes or decisions are already business critical, and where does operational confidence lag experimentation?
  2. Where does a model become “someone else's problem” today - handoff, release, monitoring, data change, or incident response?
  3. Which failure mode has most shaped the current architecture: data reliability, drift, release friction, cost, auditability, or adoption?
  4. What authority should this role have to stop a release, require controls, or trigger rollback?
  5. How are model performance, workflow adoption, and business outcomes reconciled when they point in different directions?
  6. Are you hiring primarily for deep MLOps pedigree, or for a leader who can build the operating system around a technically strong platform organization?

Discovery hypotheses

Likely pressure points to test, not assert: fragmented ownership at DS/DE/platform/business handoffs; uneven model observability; platform standardization decisions; business-metric linkage; intervention and rollback authority; cloud/evaluation economics; adoption burden; and talent/capability mix.
